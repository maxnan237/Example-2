## 2. Enrollment Trends

### Total U.S. higher education

- Fall 2025: ~19.4M students (+1% YoY) — third consecutive year of modest increases
- Private 4-year enrollment: -1.6%
- For-profit enrollment: -2.0%
- **Demographic cliff starting 2026**: 18-year-old high school graduates peaked at 3.9M in 2025; 15-year decline projected

### STRA positioning

STRA serves primarily working adults (average age ~33), insulating it somewhat from the traditional-age demographic decline. However, employer partnerships and online delivery put it in direct competition with:
- State universities expanding online programs
- Nonprofit online leaders (SNHU, WGU, Arizona State)
- Community colleges offering bachelor's degree pathways

## 4. Regulatory Environment

### Title IV / 90/10 Rule

Both Capella and Strayer participate in federal Title IV student aid programs. Proprietary institutions must derive no more than 90% of revenue from federal funds. STRA's FY2024 actuals:
- **Capella: 67.88%** (safe margin)
- **Strayer: 89.64%** (close to ceiling)

### One Big Beautiful Bill Act (July 2025)

Signed by Trump admin. Key changes for STRA:
- Eliminates Grad PLUS program (July 2026) — reduces grad student borrowing capacity
- New annual grad borrowing caps: $20,500/year, $100K aggregate
- Eliminates IDR plans (SAVE, PAYE, ICR) — replaces with Standard + RAP
- **Expands Pell Grant to short programs** — potentially positive for Sophia Learning

*(Note: OBBBA details from FSA Partners, Education Department — partially mentioned in STRA filing)*

### Gainful Employment Rule

Biden-era rule (final Sept 2023) requires career programs to prove graduates earn above non-college threshold. Failure in 2 of 3 consecutive years = Title IV loss. Trump admin is (surprisingly) **defending the Biden rule in court** as of late 2025.

For STRA: No programs have failed the test. Three were in the "warning zone" in 2015 metrics but remained compliant.

### Borrower Defense to Repayment (BDTR)

**~8,640 applications pending** across Capella (6,770) + Strayer (1,870). Material contingent liability if DOE grants relief.

## 5. AI Disruption

Education is experiencing AI transformation on multiple fronts:

### AI as cost advantage (STRA benefit)

[External sources]: STRA delivered $30M in AI-driven expense reductions in FY2025, expects $70M+ by 2027. This includes:
- Automated student support
- AI-powered course content generation
- Adaptive learning in Sophia
- Administrative efficiency

### AI as disruption threat

- 85% of students used AI tools in 2024-25 school year
- "Metacognitive laziness" — AI may reduce learning engagement
- Alternative credentials gaining traction — employers accepting Google Certificates, LinkedIn Learning badges, industry certifications in lieu of degrees
- If AI-generated credentials achieve parity with degrees, the "degree premium" compresses

### Industry response

Universities increasingly partnering with AI companies (OpenAI, Google, Microsoft). Academic integrity becoming harder to maintain. Long-term question: does AI enhance education (personalized tutoring) or replace it (direct skill assessment)?

## 6. Employer-Sponsored Education Market

This is the sub-industry where STRA has a particularly strong competitive position.

[STRA_10K_FY2025.htm]:
> "Our employer network continues to grow, with employer-affiliated enrollment now comprising more than 32% of enrollments within our USHE segment."

**Market dynamics:**
- 62% of online learners enrolled in industry-specific certifications
- 69% of employers prioritize skill-based credentials
- Major employers (Walmart, Amazon, Chipotle, Target) offer tuition benefits to attract/retain workers
- Tax-advantaged tuition reimbursement ($5,250/year tax-free under Section 127) makes this an HR retention tool

**STRA's positioning:**
- 80 employer partners via Workforce Edge covering ~3.9M employees
- Employer-funded revenue bypasses 90/10 Rule concerns
- Sophia Learning provides low-cost entry point for employer programs
- Higher persistence from employer-sponsored students

This is the cleanest growth vector in STRA's portfolio and the moat most resistant to AI disruption (B2B relationship-driven).

---

## Key Takeaway

STRA operates in a **structurally challenged industry** (post-secondary for-profit education) with secular enrollment headwinds, regulatory scrutiny, and emerging AI-based credentialing threats. However, the **employer-sponsored education sub-segment is growing** and STRA has a differentiated position through Workforce Edge. The near-term industry outlook is mixed — the demographic cliff begins in 2026, OBBBA reshapes student loan dynamics, AI creates both cost advantages and disruption risks. Companies that successfully pivot from traditional B2C enrollment to B2B employer partnerships (like STRA's ETS segment) are best positioned to outperform.
