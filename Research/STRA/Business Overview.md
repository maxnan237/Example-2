# Strategic Education Inc (STRA) — Business Overview

*Analysis date: 2026-04-12 | Primary source: FY2025 10-K (period ending Dec 31, 2025)*

---

## 1. What the Company Does

Strategic Education is a for-profit post-secondary education company that operates two U.S. universities (Strayer, Capella), an Australian university (Torrens), and an Education Technology Services segment including Sophia Learning (low-cost online courses) and Workforce Edge (employer education benefits platform).

[STRA_10K_FY2025.htm, Item 1]:
> "Strategic Education, Inc. is an education services company that provides access to high-quality education through campus-based and online post-secondary education offerings, as well as through programs to develop job-ready skills for high-demand markets."

**Corporate structure:** Formed in 2018 via merger of Strayer Education and Capella Education Company. Added ANZ business (Torrens University, Think Education) in 2020. HQ: Herndon, VA. **6,134 total employees** (3,683 full-time, 2,451 part-time, primarily adjunct faculty).

---

## 2. Business Segments

### U.S. Higher Education (USHE) — 68% of revenue

**Strayer University** (founded 1892) + **Capella University** (founded 1993). Both accredited, Title IV-eligible institutions serving working adult students.

**Student demographics (Fall 2025):** 70% age 31+, 72% part-time, 75% female, 61% minorities.

### Australia/New Zealand (ANZ) — 20% of revenue

**Torrens University Australia** (founded 2013) — "the only investor-funded University in Australia." **Think Education** (vocational training). **Media Design School** (New Zealand, creative technology). Post-Sept 2025, MDS is wholly-owned subsidiary of Strayer.

### Education Technology Services (ETS) — 12% of revenue

**Sophia Learning** — Subscription-based online general education courses transferable to 100+ institutions. 41% revenue growth in FY2025.

**Workforce Edge** — Employer education benefits administration platform. **80 employer partners** as of Dec 2025.

### Segment financials (FY2025)

| Segment | Revenue | YoY Growth | Op Income | Op Margin |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| USHE | $868M | +1.2% | $102M | 11.7% |
| ANZ | $252M | -2.2% | $35M | 14.1% |
| **ETS** | **$148M** | **+41.4%** | **$59M** | **39.6%** |
| **Total** | **$1,268M** | **+4.0%** | **$174M** | **13.7%** |

ETS is the growth engine — 41% revenue growth at 40% operating margins, up from 9% of total revenue in FY2022 to 12% in FY2025.

## 3. Revenue Model

[STRA_10K_FY2025.htm, MD&A]:
> "Approximately 94% of our revenues during the year ended December 31, 2025 consisted of tuition revenue."

Tuition recognized ratably over the course period. Pricing:
- **Strayer**: per-course fees, varies by degree level
- **Capella GuidedPath**: per-course fees on quarterly schedule
- **Capella FlexPath**: flat fee per 12-week subscription (competency-based)
- **Sophia Learning**: monthly subscription ($79/month per external sources)

### Tuition funding sources (Strayer + Capella, 90/10 Rule actuals)

| Institution | FY2024 Federal % | FY2024 Non-Federal % |
|---|---|---|
| Capella | 67.88% | 32.12% |
| Strayer | **89.64%** | 10.36% |

Strayer is close to the 90% Title IV ceiling — a key regulatory risk.

## 5. Corporate Partnerships

[STRA_10K_FY2025.htm, ETS Segment]:
> "Our employer network continues to grow, with employer-affiliated enrollment now comprising more than 32% of enrollments within our USHE segment."

**80 Workforce Edge employer partners** covering ~3.9M employees. Employers fund employee education as a talent retention benefit; STRA receives direct payments (bypassing federal aid), which:
- Reduces 90/10 compliance risk
- Improves student persistence (employer commitment)
- Creates B2B revenue stability vs. B2C marketing-dependent enrollment

## 6. Competitive Landscape

[STRA_10K_FY2025.htm, Competition]:

### Competitive dynamics

- **Free/low-cost public alternatives** — state universities and community colleges offer online programs at lower cost
- **Nonprofit online universities** (SNHU, WGU) benefit from tax advantages and lower regulatory scrutiny
- **MOOCs and direct-to-consumer** education (Coursera, edX) offer alternatives to degrees

## 7. Key Dependencies

### Title IV federal student aid

Strayer at 89.64% of revenue from federal funds creates a narrow compliance margin. Any regulatory action restricting Title IV eligibility would be material.

### Regulatory accreditation

Capella (HLC accreditation through 2032-33) and Strayer (Middle States through 2026 reaffirmation). Loss of accreditation = loss of Title IV eligibility = business failure.

### Technology infrastructure

Online delivery requires continuous tech investment. AI is both cost-reduction lever ($30M saved in FY2025, $70M+ expected through 2027) and disruption risk.

### Student enrollment demand

USHE growth slowed to -1.4% in FY2025. Growth is now dependent on employer partnerships (ETS) offsetting traditional B2C enrollment decline.

### Demographic cliff

18-year-old high school graduates peaked in 2025; 15-year decline projected. STRA is somewhat insulated (avg student age ~33, working adult focus), but competes for the same pool as traditional colleges downgrading to online.

### Regulatory action on for-profit education

CFPB scrutiny, gainful employment rules, borrower defense applications (~8,640 pending between Capella + Strayer) all pose material risks.
