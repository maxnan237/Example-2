# Uber Technologies (UBER) — Bear Case

*Analysis date: 2026-04-11 | Sources: FY2024 10-K, external data*

---

## 1. Thesis Killer: Autonomous Vehicles Could Disintermediate the Platform

Uber's core business is connecting riders with human Drivers. If autonomous vehicles eliminate the need for human Drivers, Uber either becomes the AV platform aggregator (bull case) or gets bypassed by AV operators who go direct to consumers (bear case).

**Waymo is already showing the path to disintermediation:**
- Waymo operates its own consumer-facing app in SF, LA, and Phoenix — no Uber needed
- The Uber-Waymo partnership (Austin, Atlanta) shows signs of strategic tension as Waymo expands independently
- Tesla, Zoox (Amazon), and others are developing competing AV platforms

If AV operators decide they don't need Uber's demand network — because AVs can be deployed at lower cost than human Drivers and riders will go wherever the cheapest/best ride is — Uber's 27% take rate becomes vulnerable.

**The timing question:** This is a 5-10 year risk, not a near-term threat. But the market is already pricing some AV optionality into Uber's stock (Rivian deal, Nuro partnership, Nvidia collaboration). If these bets don't work, the premium evaporates.

---

## 2. Structural Weaknesses

### 2a. Low switching costs — Uber's own admission

> "Low barriers to entry, low switching costs, and well-capitalized competitors in nearly every major geographic region."

Riders download Lyft in 30 seconds. Drivers multi-app simultaneously. Merchants list on DoorDash AND Uber Eats. The platform has scale but no lock-in beyond Uber One.

### 2b. Driver classification — existential regulatory risk

> "Our business would be adversely affected if Drivers were classified as employees."

150,000+ Drivers have filed arbitration demands. If reclassified as employees in major markets, Uber would owe benefits, payroll taxes, minimum wage guarantees, and overtime — fundamentally changing the cost structure and potentially making the business unprofitable.

### 2c. U.S. Delivery is #2 behind DoorDash

DoorDash holds ~60-67% of U.S. food delivery. Uber Eats has ~23%. Uber is the clear #2 and has not been able to close the gap. In the most important delivery market in the world, Uber loses on share.

### 2d. Freight is a value destroyer

Freight revenue declined 26% from FY2022 ($6.9B) to FY2024 ($5.1B) and has been unprofitable (($74M) Adj. EBITDA in FY2024). It's unclear why Uber should be in freight brokerage — it doesn't leverage the consumer network.

### 2e. Insurance reserves are ballooning

$9.8B in insurance reserves (FY2024) vs. $4.7B (FY2022) — more than doubled. Auto liability claims are a growing cost that may not be fully reserved.

---

## 3. Assumptions the Bull Case Takes for Granted

1. **Network effects are durable.** If AV operators go direct, or a well-funded competitor (Amazon?) enters ridesharing, network effects could erode faster than expected.

2. **Take rate stays at 27-30%.** Regulatory pressure, Driver organizing, and competition could compress take rates. A 2-point take rate decline on $163B in Gross Bookings = ~$3.3B in lost revenue.

3. **Uber One growth continues.** At 36M members, growth may slow as the easiest-to-acquire users have already joined. If member growth stalls, the switching cost narrative weakens.

4. **Advertising grows to $5B+.** The $1.6B → $5B trajectory requires merchants to significantly increase ad spend on the platform. Competitive pressure from DoorDash's advertising platform and general macro ad spending sensitivity could slow this.

5. **Driver supply stays abundant.** If alternative employment improves (strong labor market) or gig work regulations tighten, Driver supply could constrain growth.

---

## 4. Permanent Impairment Scenarios

### AV disintermediation
Waymo/Tesla/Zoox launch direct-to-consumer AV ridesharing at lower prices, bypassing Uber's platform. Uber's demand network loses relevance as AVs don't need dynamic matching — they're centrally dispatched. Timeline: 5-10 years but accelerating.

### Global Driver reclassification
EU, UK, and U.S. states pass legislation requiring Drivers to be classified as employees with full benefits. Uber's cost per trip rises 20-30%, making many routes unprofitable and forcing service area contraction.

### DoorDash takes delivery share to 80%+
DoorDash continues gaining U.S. share, reaching dominant position where merchants prioritize DoorDash listing. Uber Eats becomes an afterthought in the U.S., forcing margin-destroying promotions to maintain relevance.

## Summary

The bear case is: **Uber operates a marketplace with self-acknowledged low switching costs and low barriers to entry, dependent on millions of independent contractors whose classification is under constant legal challenge, while autonomous vehicle technology threatens to eliminate the need for those contractors entirely — and in the company's most important delivery market, it's a distant #2.** The current ~15x P/E prices in continued execution, but the 5-10 year structural risks (AV, regulation) are genuinely existential and difficult to handicap.
